Photos, Dreams, and the Upload Button

A Christmas night, a Lisbon canteen by the sea, and the quiet power of a single post.

Christmas, Observador, and the Glow of a Screen

It’s Christmas. The house is quiet.

I’ve just finished reading an article on my computer — an Observador piece about European regulators, tech companies, and retaliation. Heavy stuff. The Digital Services Act (DSA). Power. Platforms. Consequences.

To my left, my wife scrolls slowly through photos — Christmas dinners, smiling families, clinking glasses, kids half-asleep on sofas. Little moments, captured and shared without much thought.

I wonder how many of these people know about the Digital Services Act. Or image rights. Or how thin the line really is between memory and exposure.

I smile at her. She smiles back — warm, familiar, safe.

The bedside lamp clicks off. Darkness. Sleep.

The Dream Begins

I don’t know how long I’ve been asleep when I hear a voice.

At first it feels far away, like it’s coming through a tunnel.

“João… João… wake up.”

A hand on my shoulder. Gentle.

I wake up, heart racing, skin damp.

“What? What’s wrong?”

“You were having a nightmare,” she says softly. “Are you okay?”

I hesitate, then blurt out:

“Did you post those photos we took at the restaurant on Facebook?”

She blinks. “No. Why?”

Relief floods in. I turn over. Sleep takes me again.

The Canteen by the Sea

This time, the dream is different.

A boy — about 17 — sits in a canteen overlooking the sea in Lisbon. Across from him is his girlfriend, Maria, a year younger. School bags at their feet. Espresso cups between them. Phones on the table.

It’s after a festival.

Music still ringing in their ears.

Maria leans in. “That photo you took of me dancing — it’s cute. Are you posting it?”

The boy grins. “Of course. Everyone was there.”

“It’s a Public Festival, Relax”

He’s already pinching the screen, framing the shot.

Maria hesitates. “Wait… can you actually do that?”

He laughs. “It’s a public festival. We paid for tickets.”

Reality check: In Portugal, taking a photo and posting it online are two very different legal acts.

The Espresso Rule

“If I wouldn’t show it to them over coffee, I won’t post it.”

Maria smiles. “That’s actually smart.”

What the Law in Portugal Actually Says (Plain English)

You don’t need to be a lawyer to stay out of trouble. These are the key legal points anyone taking or posting photos in Portugal should understand.

1. Taking a Photo Is Not the Same as Posting It

  • You can usually take photos in public places (streets, parks, festivals).
  • Posting or sharing a photo is a separate legal act.
  • Publishing someone’s image without consent can violate their right to image.

2. Public Place Does NOT Mean “Free to Post”

  • Even in public events or festivals, people keep their image rights.
  • Wide crowd shots are usually fine.
  • Close-ups or clearly identifiable people usually require consent.

3. Ask Yourself: Who Is the Subject?

  • If a person is just part of the background, posting is generally safer.
  • If a person is the main focus of the image, consent is usually required.
  • If the image could embarrass, expose, or harm someone, don’t post it.

4. Private Accounts and Groups Still Count as Sharing

  • WhatsApp groups, private Instagram accounts, Telegram chats — these still count as distribution.
  • “It’s only a few people” is not a legal defence.
  • If someone asks you to remove a photo, you should do so.

5. Minors Are Specially Protected

  • Children and teenagers have extra legal protection.
  • Publishing identifiable images of minors usually requires parental consent.
  • This applies even at public events and festivals.

6. Tickets and Signs Don’t Protect You

  • Buying a ticket does not cancel image rights.
  • Festival signs usually protect the organisers, not attendees.
  • You are still responsible for what you post.

7. You Can Be Asked to Take It Down

  • People have the right to object to the use of their image.
  • Refusing to remove content increases legal risk.
  • Most disputes are resolved simply by deleting the post.

Simple Consent Examples You Can Actually Use

Consent doesn’t have to be complicated or formal. In most everyday situations, a clear and honest message is enough. Below are practical examples you can copy, adapt, or screenshot for your own use.

1. Simple Everyday Consent (Friends, Casual Photos)

Best for: dinners, meetups, festivals with friends

“Hey! I took a photo/video where you appear. Are you okay if I post it on Instagram/Facebook?”

If the answer is “yes”, keep the message. If it’s “no” or silence — don’t post.

---

2. Consent for Posting Festival or Event Photos (Non-Commercial)

Best for: public events, festivals, community gatherings

“I’m happy to be photographed or filmed at this event and I agree to the photo/video being shared on social media for non-commercial purposes.”

This works best when the person is clearly identifiable and is the main subject of the image.

---

3. Consent Limited to Private Groups

Best for: WhatsApp, Telegram, private Facebook or Instagram accounts

“I’m okay with this photo/video being shared in this private group, but not on public social networks.”

This makes boundaries clear and protects both sides.

---

4. Consent for Photos Involving Minors (Parents or Guardians)

Important: Images of children and teenagers require extra care.

“I, as the parent/legal guardian of [child’s name], give permission for photos/videos taken on [date/place] to be shared for non-commercial purposes.”

When in doubt, don’t post images of minors — especially if they are identifiable.

---

5. Consent for Promotional or Commercial Use

Best for: blogs, business pages, marketing, monetised content

“I agree to the use of my image in photos/videos taken on [date/place] for promotional or commercial purposes, including websites and social media.”

For commercial use, written consent is strongly recommended.

---

6. When Someone Changes Their Mind

If someone later asks you to remove a photo or video, the safest response is also the simplest.

“Of course — I’ve removed it and won’t share it further.”

In Portugal, continuing to publish after an objection can increase legal risk.

---

A Final Word on Consent

  • Consent should be clear and specific.
  • Consent to take a photo does not automatically mean consent to post it.
  • Private sharing is still sharing.
  • When unsure, ask — or don’t post.

Back in the Room

I wake up slowly this time.

The room is quiet again. The faint glow of Lisbon outside the window. No canteen. No students. No phones on tables.

My wife is awake, turned slightly towards me.

“You were restless,” she says gently. “Another nightmare?”

I nod. “Not really a nightmare,” I say after a pause. “More like… a reminder.”

She waits. She always does.

“I was thinking about how easy it is,” I continue. “How we take photos without thinking. How we post without asking. How something small can suddenly belong to everyone.”

She smiles, soft and familiar. “You and your thoughts.”

I smile back.

“It’s strange,” I say. “We worry about big laws. Big platforms. Big power. But most of it comes down to small moments. One button. One choice.”

She reaches for my hand.

“Did you want me to delete something?” she asks.

I shake my head. “No. Just… keep doing what you already do.”

The room settles again.

Before sleep takes me, one last thought drifts through my mind — simple, human, and probably enough for most of us.

The Espresso Rule:
If I wouldn’t show it to them calmly over coffee, I probably shouldn’t post it.

The light goes out.

Closing Thought

Portugal doesn’t hate photos.

It just expects something increasingly rare online:

Pause. Awareness. Respect.
