Introduction

Scrolling through a Facebook debate about Ventura’s latest comments on the burka, I couldn’t help but be shocked by some of the opinions people were throwing around. It made me think back to his controversial posters and the bigger question: how lawful and ethical is all of this? I did some soul-searching, a bit of research, and here’s my take — my 50 cents — on the matter.

Or: how a university class in Porto accidentally wandered into one of Portugal’s most uncomfortable conversations.

The lecturer stood by the window, hands clasped behind his back, staring out across the river. The Douro flowed calmly below, catching the early sunlight like a lazy ribbon of silver. Rabelo boats drifted by as if they had nowhere urgent to be, and the opposite bank rose gently, painted in the pastel colours Porto reserves for postcards and tourists.

It should have been peaceful. But it wasn’t.

Dr. João Almeida — early sixties, black hair bravely resisting grey but clearly losing the battle — looked troubled. Not the “I forgot my glasses” troubled. The “how on earth do I explain this without starting a riot” troubled.

He sighed quietly.

Law versus ethics.
Freedom of speech versus discrimination.
Censorship versus responsibility.

Simple topics. Nothing controversial at all.

The morning bell chimed.

The hallway’s muffled roar poured into the classroom like a floodgate opening. Backpacks thumped. Chairs scraped. Pens clicked with unnecessary aggression. Conversations bloomed and overlapped until the room buzzed like a caffeinated beehive.

Then — silence.

The students noticed the look on their lecturer’s face. The serious one. The “today we are not discussing exam dates” face.

Dr. Almeida turned slowly from the window.

“Good morning, ladies and gentlemen,” he said. “Today, I am giving you this period to prepare for Tuesday’s debate.”

A hand shot up.

“Which debate, Dr. João?” asked André, already suspicious.

The lecturer smiled faintly — the smile of a man who knows chaos is coming.

The Assignment

“The class,” Dr. Almeida explained, “will be divided into two groups.”

“One group will argue that André Ventura is acting within his legal right to freedom of expression with his posters and public statements.”

“The other group will examine Portuguese law and ethics, and argue whether those same actions cross legal or ethical boundaries.”

A pause.

“And,” he added, “you must remember: legality and ethics are not the same thing.”

A few students nodded. Some frowned. One quietly googled “what is ethics.”

Fast Forward to Tuesday

Two speakers from each group stood at the front of the class.

André — yes, that André — was representing Ventura’s position. The coincidence was not lost on the lecturer, who smiled at the universe’s sense of humour.

Across from him stood Nicole, armed with notes, case law, and the unmistakable confidence of someone who had read more than just the headlines.

The debate began.

André Speaks: “It’s Just Free Speech”

André cleared his throat.

“Freedom of expression is protected by Article 37 of the Portuguese Constitution,” he began. “Political speech, especially, has strong protection.”

He gestured dramatically.

“When Ventura says things like ‘Os ciganos têm de cumprir a lei’ or ‘As minorias do costume têm de cumprir a lei’, he is stating something technically true. Everyone must obey the law.”

“That’s not a call to violence. It’s not saying anyone should lose rights. It’s not censorship-worthy. You can’t ban speech just because it makes people uncomfortable.”

André paused, satisfied.

“This is freedom of speech. And in a democracy, that freedom includes speech we don’t like.”

Nicole Responds: “Law Is More Than Literal Words”

Nicole stepped forward.

“Yes, freedom of expression exists,” she said. “But it is not absolute.”

“In Portugal, freedom of expression is balanced against other constitutional rights: human dignity, equality, and non-discrimination.”

She tapped her notes.

“When a poster singles out a specific ethnic group — like Roma people — and implies they are uniquely responsible for breaking the law, it reinforces stereotypes.”

“That’s why courts don’t just look at what is said, but how it’s said, who says it, and what effect it has.”

“Nicole continued:”

“A Lisbon court ordered these posters removed because they were found to be discriminatory. That means: in this case, the speech was ruled not protected.”

Law vs Ethics (This Is Where It Gets Interesting)

Dr. Almeida leaned back in his chair, clearly enjoying this part.

Nicole continued:

“Even when speech is legal, it may still be unethical.”

“Ethics asks different questions than the law. Not ‘Can I say this?’ but ‘Should I say this?’”

“As a party leader and influencer, Ventura’s words carry weight. He’s not just expressing an opinion — he’s shaping public perception.”

“When you repeatedly highlight one minority group as a problem, you create social harm, even without explicit insults or violence.”

“That’s why many people say this kind of language is irresponsible — even when it skirts legality.”

So… Is Ventura Acting Legally?

The answer, inconveniently, is: sometimes.

  • ✔ Saying “everyone must obey the law” → legal
  • ✔ Criticising policies → legal
  • ✖ Singling out an ethnic group in a way that reinforces prejudice → courts may intervene

In at least one case, the courts did intervene. That means: not everything he did stayed within the law.

And Ethically?

This is where opinions diverge — but many agree on one thing:

Ethical leadership requires care, restraint, and awareness of impact.

Ventura’s supporters may see blunt honesty. Critics see calculated provocation.

But few would argue that repeatedly targeting minorities promotes unity or social trust.

The Lecturer’s Closing Thought

Dr. Almeida stood once more by the window, the Douro still flowing patiently outside.

“The law,” he said, “tells us what is permitted.”

“Ethics,” he added, “tells us what is right.”

“And democracy,” he concluded, “depends on us understanding the difference.”

The room was quiet.

Some students nodded. Some frowned. Some reconsidered their social media posts.

Outside, Porto carried on — unchanged, complex, and very much alive.
