The Truth About Losing Winter Weight

I was sitting in front of the television at my daughter’s apartment. The news anchor was speaking in that serious voice they reserve for major global events.

War in Iran… rising fuel prices… energy shortages… food prices climbing…

The room had that quiet tension you get when the world seems slightly unstable.

Spring, however, was just around the corner — and judging by the conversation behind me, global politics wasn’t the only concern on people’s minds.

“The best way is to join a gym and sweat it out,” my daughter’s friend declared confidently.

My daughter replied, “Yeah, but I heard weights are actually better than cardio.”

Just then the TV flashed: Trump about to speak…

“Dad, dad! Did you hear us?” my daughter asked.

“Ha? What?” I replied, turning away from the TV.

“Resistance training or cardio — which is best for weight loss?”

I paused for a moment, smiled, and said:

“NEAT.”

Both of them stared at me like I had just invented a new cryptocurrency.

“NEAT? What is that uncle? Is it some new type of exercise?” her friend asked.

“Sort of,” I replied. “For some people it is exercise… for others it’s just life.”

I looked at their confused faces and said:

“Make me a coffee and come sit here so I can explain.”

The Truth About Weight Loss

It’s All About Energy

Most fad diets, miracle supplements, and extreme exercise plans ignore one simple reality:

Your body weight is largely controlled by energy balance.

In simple terms:

  • Calories in (food)
  • Calories out (energy your body burns)

The “calories out” part is called Total Daily Energy ExpenditureTDEE.

And this is where our four characters come into the story:

  • BMR
  • NEAT
  • EAT
  • TEF

Think of them like the four roommates living inside your metabolism.

Meet the Metabolic Roommates

BMR — The Lazy One

BMR stands for Basal Metabolic Rate.

This is the energy your body burns doing absolutely nothing.

Breathing. Circulating blood. Keeping organs running. Keeping you alive while watching Netflix.

BMR typically accounts for about 60–70% of the calories you burn every day.

And here’s the interesting part:

Muscle increases BMR.

Lean muscle mass burns more calories than fat even at rest.

So the more muscle you have, the slightly faster your metabolism runs — even when you are sitting.

“That’s why resistance training matters,” I told them.

NEAT — The Sneaky One

NEAT stands for Non-Exercise Activity Thermogenesis.

And this one is fascinating.

NEAT includes:

  • walking
  • standing
  • cleaning
  • fidgeting
  • taking stairs
  • gardening
  • playing with the dog

Basically all movement that is not exercise.

Studies show NEAT can vary by hundreds or even thousands of calories per day between people.

Some people naturally move all day long.

Others sit… a lot.

“Which means,” I told them,

“Someone who never goes to the gym can still burn more calories than someone who does.”

Both girls blinked at me.

“Yes,” I said. “That’s why NEAT is so powerful.”

EAT — The Gym Lover

EAT stands for Exercise Activity Thermogenesis.

This is traditional exercise:

  • running
  • cycling
  • gym workouts
  • sports

Exercise is important for health.

But surprisingly, it usually represents only about 10–30% of calories burned daily.

Which shocked my daughter’s friend.

“So the gym isn't everything?” she asked.

“No,” I said. “But it still helps a lot.”

TEF — The Hardworking One

TEF is the Thermic Effect of Food.

Your body actually burns calories digesting food.

But not all foods cost the same energy to process.

  • Protein: burns about 20–30% during digestion
  • Carbohydrates: about 5–10%
  • Fat: about 0–3%

“So protein wins,” my daughter said.

“Exactly,” I replied.

Protein:

  • burns more calories during digestion
  • keeps you full longer
  • helps maintain muscle

Cardio vs Resistance Training

“Okay,” my daughter said. “So which is better?”

I took a sip of coffee.

“Both.”

Cardio

Cardio burns more calories during the workout itself.

Running, cycling, swimming — they burn energy fast.

Resistance Training

Resistance training builds muscle.

That helps:

  • maintain metabolism
  • improve body composition
  • prevent muscle loss during dieting

And then there is something called the afterburn effect.

After intense training, the body continues burning extra calories for hours while recovering.

“So weights keep burning calories even after the workout,” my daughter said.

“Exactly.”

Sleep

The Secret Weapon

Then I asked them a question.

“How much sleep do you get?”

They looked at each other.

“Not enough,” they admitted.

“Before you two run off and start counting calories or signing up for the nearest gym,” I said, leaning back in my chair, “there’s one thing most people forget when they talk about weight loss… sleep.” Both of them looked at me with raised eyebrows. “Sleep?” my daughter asked. “You mean doing nothing actually helps you lose weight?”

“Well,” I replied, “it’s not exactly doing nothing. Your body is actually very busy while you sleep.” Research over the past decade has shown that sleep plays a surprisingly important role in weight management. In one well-known study published in the journal Annals of Internal Medicine, researchers found that people who slept about 8.5 hours per night during a calorie-restricted diet lost significantly more fat than those who slept only about 5.5 hours. The short-sleep group still lost weight, but nearly half of that loss came from lean muscle instead of body fat — something you definitely want to avoid if you're trying to keep your metabolism healthy.

The reason lies partly in hormones. When we don’t get enough sleep, the body increases levels of ghrelin, the hormone that signals hunger, and reduces leptin, the hormone that tells us we are full. The result? We feel hungrier the next day and are more likely to crave high-calorie foods. At the same time, poor sleep can reduce insulin sensitivity and lower the amount of spontaneous movement we do during the day — what scientists call NEAT (Non-Exercise Activity Thermogenesis). In other words, lack of sleep can make us eat more while also burning fewer calories.

“So,” I said, pointing at the two of them, “if you’re serious about fitting into those summer outfits, don’t just think about diets or workouts. Think about your bedtime too.” Getting around 7 to 9 hours of good quality sleep each night helps regulate appetite hormones, supports recovery after exercise, and keeps your metabolism functioning properly. It might not feel like a traditional weight-loss strategy, but in many ways it’s one of the easiest — and most overlooked — ways to help your body burn fat more effectively.

Poor sleep:

  • increases hunger hormones
  • reduces metabolic activity
  • lowers daily movement

Getting 7–9 hours of sleep helps regulate appetite and energy balance.

What If You Hate Exercise?

My daughter’s friend raised her hand.

“What if someone doesn’t like exercise?”

“No problem,” I said.

Weight loss can still happen through:

  • increasing NEAT (walking, chores, standing)
  • higher protein intake
  • better sleep
  • reducing long sitting periods

Simply moving more throughout the day can increase calorie burn by 300–600 calories daily.

Without a single gym session.

Most Effective Strategy for Weight Loss or Maintenance

Research consistently shows the best approach combines:

  • moderate calorie control
  • resistance training
  • cardio
  • high daily movement
  • adequate protein
  • good sleep

Not extreme dieting.

Not endless cardio.

Understanding how energy works.

  • Reduce stress
  • Stay consistent

My Final Thoughts

The girls finished their coffee.

The news was still talking about oil prices and geopolitics.

Spring was still on its way.

And summer outfits were clearly still on their minds.

I looked at them and said:

“The problem with most diets is that they treat weight loss like a short-term project. But the body is a system. Once you understand how energy works — BMR, NEAT, TEF and exercise — everything becomes simpler.”

You don’t need extreme fasting.

You don’t need to live in the gym.

You just need to understand how your body spends energy — and gently tip the balance in your favour.

And if you ask me?

The real secret is not quick weight loss before summer.

It’s building habits that work all year long.
