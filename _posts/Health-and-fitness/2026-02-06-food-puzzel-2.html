Part 2 of the series: The Upside-Down Food Pyramid

Picking Up Where We Left Off

In the previous post, I shared how a simple coffee conversation led me down a much deeper rabbit hole — one that connected childhood diabetes, protein intake, wheat fields in Portugal, and rice farms in Mozambique.

We ended with a question:

What if the food pyramid was never meant to make us healthier?

In this post, we zoom out — away from the café table — and rewind to the moment when modern nutrition policy was born.

Not in a laboratory.

Not in a hospital.

But in the middle of a world at war.

The Coffee Arrives — and the Explanation Continues

The waitress returns with our drinks.

A bica for my wife. A galão for me.

So,” she says, stirring her coffee, “you were saying this all started after the war?”

“Exactly,” I reply. “And once you see it, it’s hard to unsee.”

I take a sip and continue.

“During the Second World War, governments weren’t asking how to optimise health. They were asking something far more basic.”

How do we feed millions of people — cheaply, predictably, and at scale?

WWII Logistics: Calories First, Biology Later

World War II forced governments to think about food in entirely new ways.

Millions of soldiers were deployed overseas. Entire civilian populations were under rationing. Supply chains were fragile. Spoilage was a real threat.

The priority wasn’t optimal nutrition.

The priority was:

  • Calories per unit of cost
  • Shelf stability
  • Ease of transport and storage
  • Predictable production

From a logistical standpoint, carbohydrates were unbeatable.

Grains could be dried, stored, shipped, and portioned with minimal loss. Wheat, rice, corn, and sugar delivered energy reliably — and cheaply.

Protein and fat, on the other hand, were expensive, perishable, and harder to manage at scale.

So the system did what systems do.

It optimised for survival.

Rations, Not Recommendations

Early nutrition guidelines were not lifestyle advice.

They were rationing tools.

They answered questions like:

  • How many calories does a soldier need to keep fighting?
  • How little protein prevents visible deficiency?
  • What foods can be standardised across populations?

This is where the now-famous 0.8 grams of protein per kilogram originates — not as an optimal target, but as a bare minimum to prevent deficiency in young, active men.

It was never designed for:

  • Aging populations
  • Sedentary lifestyles
  • Metabolic disease
  • Muscle preservation

But once written into policy, numbers have a way of sticking around.

The Birth of Population-Level Nutrition

After the war, governments didn’t abandon these frameworks.

They institutionalised them.

Nutrition moved from emergency logistics into public policy — school lunches, hospitals, military food systems, and eventually national dietary guidelines.

The focus remained the same:

Feed the population efficiently.

Not:

Optimise individual health.

This distinction matters more than most people realise.

Why Carbohydrates Won — Politically

Carbohydrates didn’t just win because they were practical.

They won because they aligned with:

  • Agricultural subsidies
  • Industrial food production
  • Post-war economic rebuilding
  • Centralised planning

Grain-based systems could be scaled, controlled, and measured.

Protein — especially animal protein — was messy. Variable. Local. Harder to standardise.

So the food pyramid emerged not from biology, but from bureaucracy.

A neat diagram that reflected policy priorities, not human physiology.

The Long Shadow of Wartime Thinking

What began as a wartime necessity slowly transformed into nutritional dogma.

Calories became king.

Carbohydrates became the base.

Protein became something to “add later.”

And fat? Something to fear.

The result is a framework that made sense in 1945 — but looks increasingly out of place in a world facing obesity, diabetes, and muscle loss.

My Perspective

As an accredited CPD Fitness, Health, Nutrition, and Wellness Coach, I see the downstream effects of these policies every day.

Guidelines designed for population survival are still being treated as blueprints for individual health — despite living in a completely different metabolic reality.

Understanding where these ideas came from is not about blame.

It’s about recognising when a system has outlived its original purpose.
