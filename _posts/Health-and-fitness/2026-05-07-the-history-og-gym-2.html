At Snow Haven having Milkshakes

A Saturday Morning Conversation at Now Haven, Welkom

It’s a lazy Saturday morning at Now Haven, that legendary ice-cream-and-burger joint tucked into what we locals affectionately call "the Hoefie" — the horseshoe-shaped heart of Welkom, with the park lounging in the middle and businesses hugging the curve outside.

Chris and I are seated with thick chocolate milkshakes sweating in the Free State heat, when I casually drop:

Me: "You know, many moons ago athletes trained out in the woods… completely nude."
Chris: (laughing) "Ja right. You’re making that up."

That’s when I explain that ancient gyms didn’t just shape bodies — they shaped minds. That philosophers like Plato and Aristotle trained alongside athletes. Chris listens, curious now. He’d heard about our gym sessions and was quietly wondering if he might join.

Right on cue, a familiar voice interrupts.

Joseph: "Hi guys."

He’d recognised me immediately.

Joseph: "Who’s your friend?"
Me: "This is Chris. He’s interested in joining the gym — we were just talking about the history of gyms and the mind–body connection."

Before Chris can reply, the waitress arrives, placing Joseph’s order on the table: oats, three poached eggs, and a large chicken breast. Chris stares. My milkshake suddenly feels very exposed.

Joseph: (smiling) "Mind if I join you? I remember you wanted to know about the men who influenced the history of gyms."

The Conversation Begins: Strength Before Gyms

Joseph: "Before buildings called gyms existed, strength lived in stories. Think of Hercules — or Heracles. His Twelve Labours weren’t about looking good. They were about discipline, endurance, and moral testing."

Chris nods slowly, straw paused mid-air.

Joseph: "These myths set the template. Strength wasn’t vanity — it was virtue."

Ancient Greece: Training the Body and the Brain

Joseph: "Now, about the nude training — that part is true. Greek athletes trained naked in the gymnasium. The word itself comes from gymnos, meaning naked."
Chris: "You’re serious?"
Joseph: "Very. But the gymnasium wasn’t just a workout space. It was a school. Philosophers like Aristotle taught there. The idea was simple: a strong body supports a strong mind."

He mentions Milo of Croton, the legendary wrestler who carried a calf every day as it grew into a bull — an early, accidental lesson in progressive overload.

Rome: From Philosophy to Spectacle

Joseph: "The Romans took Greek training and made it practical — and theatrical. Gladiators trained in special schools called ludi. Soldiers drilled relentlessly with bodyweight movements, loaded marches, and weapons."

Chris glances at Joseph’s chicken breast.

Chris: "So… functional training isn’t new?"
Joseph: "Not at all. We’re just rediscovering it."

Strongmen, Scripture, and Survival

Joseph: "Across cultures, strength carried meaning. In the Bible, Samson represented divinely granted power. Lose the discipline — and the strength disappears."

Strength, Joseph explains, was tied to survival, morality, and identity long before it was tied to mirrors.

Circuses, Strongmen & the Birth of Modern Physique

Joseph: "Fast forward to the 19th century. Strongmen performed in circuses, theatres, and travelling shows long before commercial gyms became popular. Back then, muscles were entertainment."
Chris: "So basically… the first gym influencers?"
Joseph: "Except these guys didn’t just post selfies. They bent steel bars around their necks, pulled cars with their teeth, and let motorcycles drive over their chests."

Chris nearly spits out his milkshake.

Joseph: "One of the most famous was Eugen Sandow, considered the father of modern bodybuilding. He transformed strength into art and aesthetics. Before Sandow, being strong was enough. After Sandow, people wanted to look strong too."
Eugen Sandow - Wikipedia
Joseph: "Then there were performers like Ivan the Great, who once held up a platform carrying five dancing couples. Imagine leg day going that wrong."
Chris: "Five couples? My arms get tired carrying groceries."
Joseph: "André Rivière had women jump onto his stomach from tables to prove his core strength. Another strongman pulled cars with his teeth outside the U.S. Capitol. Meanwhile, steel benders like Gene Chalard amazed crowds by supporting heavy carts on their windpipes."

The waitress walks past slowly, clearly wondering what kind of conversation involves motorcycles, teeth, and windpipes before breakfast.

Joseph: "Strongmen like Jay Rolino let cars drive over their chests. Others balanced motorcycles, bent iron bars, broke chains with their legs, or held people above their heads with one arm. It sounds insane today, but these men and women became celebrities because strength represented courage, discipline, and spectacle."
Me: "So this is where bodybuilding and performance culture really exploded?"
Joseph: "Exactly. This was the birth of modern physique culture. Training became both performance and aspiration. People didn’t just admire strength anymore — they wanted to build it themselves."

For a moment, Chris looks thoughtfully at Joseph’s chicken breast and oats… then down at his milkshake.

Hollywood, Icons & the Modern Gym

Joseph: "Movies sealed the deal. Once Hollywood discovered muscles could sell tickets, gyms exploded in popularity."
Chris: "So superheroes basically built the modern gym?"
Joseph: "Pretty much. But it started long before Marvel. Back in the 1950s, actors like Steve Reeves played Hercules with that classic Greek-statue physique. He looked powerful without looking like he could bench-press a truck."
Steve Reeves Workout – Old School Aesthetics

Joseph pauses long enough to spear a piece of chicken while Chris studies his own skinny arms with growing concern.

Joseph: "Then came the golden age of bodybuilding. Gold’s Gym in California became the unofficial headquarters of muscle culture. Guys like Arnold Schwarzenegger and Lou Ferrigno trained there daily, turning bodybuilding from a niche hobby into worldwide entertainment."
Me: "Arnold really changed everything, didn’t he?"
Joseph: "Absolutely. Arnold wasn’t just a bodybuilder — he became a movie star. Suddenly every teenager wanted biceps after watching Conan the Barbarian and The Terminator. Even gym posters became motivational speeches with abs."
Arnold (left) and Lou (right) Shaking Hands with Ben Weider
Chris: "I blame those movies for every guy trying curls in front of mirrors."
Joseph: "Then the 1980s and 90s took things even further. Sylvester Stallone made people want to train like boxers after Rocky. Suddenly everyone was sprinting up stairs and punching hanging meat in butcher shops."

I nearly choke on my milkshake laughing.

Joseph: "After that came the era of the massive action heroes — guys like Dwayne ‘The Rock’ Johnson. His personal travelling gym, called The Iron Paradise, literally moves around the world with him. Imagine needing forty thousand pounds of equipment just to avoid missing chest day."
Chris: "My entire room weighs less than that."
Joseph: "Today actors train year-round because superhero movies demand ridiculous transformations. Chris Hemsworth trained like a Norse god for Thor. John Boyega leaned down and built athletic muscle for action roles. Modern Hollywood expects stars to look ready to save the planet at any moment."
Me: "And now ordinary people chase those physiques too."
Joseph: "Exactly. Modern gyms became a blend of everything history created — bodybuilding, sport, rehabilitation, aesthetics, mental discipline, and lifestyle. Today you’ll find heavy weights next to yoga mats, cryotherapy chambers next to squat racks, and influencers filming workouts between sets."

Joseph leans back slightly and grins.

Joseph: "From ancient Greek gymnasiums to superhero movie contracts… humans have always admired strength. The only difference now is we have protein shakes, LED lighting, and Wi-Fi."

Conclusion

As milkshakes empty and plates clear, one thing becomes obvious: the gym was never just about lifting weight. It has always been about lifting the human condition — physically, mentally, culturally.

Final Thoughts (From Me)

As an accredited CPD Fitness, Health, Nutrition, and Wellness Coach, I see this history play out daily. When someone trains, they’re not just chasing muscle or fat loss — they’re stepping into a lineage that stretches from ancient groves to modern gym floors.

Understanding where gyms come from helps us respect what they’re meant to be: places of growth, discipline, and balance — not punishment.
