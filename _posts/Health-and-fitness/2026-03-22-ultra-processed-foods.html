Where Muscles Are Really Built

Northern Free State, early 2000s. Summer. Around 20h00.

The gym lights flicker off one by one. Most people have already rushed home — probably to eat whatever was quickest and closest. Outside the entrance, two guys are still standing there, towels over shoulders, gym bags at their feet, completely unaware of the time.

This wasn’t unusual.

Back then, gyms weren’t just places where men trained their muscles. They were places where ideas were trained too.

Philip — buff, fair-skinned, light brown hair, thirty years old and already obsessed with nutrition — leans against the wall, arms crossed, still buzzing from the workout.

Next to him stands his gym partner — just as buff, black hair, tanned skin, born in Portugal — listening carefully while pretending he isn’t starving.

“So,” Philip says, “I listened to this podcast today. About ultra-processed foods.”

The Portuguese raises an eyebrow. “Ultra what?”

“What Even Is Processed Food?”

Philip grins. “That’s exactly how it starts. Not all processed food is bad. Processing just means food has been changed somehow — cooking, freezing, fermenting, canning. Your mom’s stew? Processed. Frozen vegetables? Processed.”

“So I don’t need to panic yet?”

“Not yet,” Philip laughs. “The problem starts with levels.”

He explains the NOVA system — four levels, from whole foods to ultra-processed foods. By the time he gets to level four, his partner is shaking his head.

“You’re telling me food can be engineered?”

“Exactly. Ultra-processed foods aren’t really ‘foods’ anymore. They’re industrial formulas — substances extracted from foods, mixed with additives you don’t use at home, designed to last forever and make you eat more.”

“How Do You Spot This Stuff?”

“Easy,” Philip says. “Flip the packet. If the ingredient list looks like a chemistry exam — emulsifiers, stabilisers, flavour enhancers — congratulations, you’re holding an ultra-processed food.”

“What about ‘healthy’ labels?”

“Marketing,” Philip smirks. “Some processed foods are fine — canned beans, frozen veggies, yoghurt with a short ingredient list. But ultra-processed foods usually break the five-ingredient rule before you even reach the bottom of the label.”

“And People Actually Eat This… Daily?”

Philip nods. “In some countries, more than half of daily calories come from ultra-processed foods. The US and UK lead the pack. Southern Europe — places like Portugal — still eat less of it, but it’s rising.”

The Portuguese sighs. “So my grandmother was right.”

“Always was,” Philip says.

“Why Is Ultra-Processed Food So Popular?”

“Because it’s cheap, convenient, tasty, and everywhere,” Philip says. “It doesn’t go off, it needs no cooking, and it’s designed to hit your brain’s reward system.”

“So it’s not lack of discipline?”

“Not entirely. It’s biology, environment, marketing — and lifestyle.”

Lifestyle, Gyms, and the Irony

Philip laughs. “Here’s the irony. People who don’t exercise much, sleep badly, or live stressful lives tend to eat more ultra-processed food. But even gym-goers fall into the trap.”

The Portuguese looks down at his shaker bottle. “Protein bars?”

Philip nods slowly. “Some of them are basically candy bars wearing gym clothes.”

The Health Bill Comes Later

“So what’s the damage?”

Philip becomes serious. “Higher risk of obesity, type 2 diabetes, heart disease, depression, even early death. The research is solid — the more ultra-processed food you eat, the worse the long-term outcomes.”

“And people still argue about it?”

“They argue because the food industry is big, loud, and profitable.”

What Are Countries Doing About It?

Philip explains how some countries tax sugary drinks, restrict marketing to kids, or add warning labels. Others are still debating.

“South Africa?” the Portuguese asks.

“We’re stuck with a double problem,” Philip says. “Undernutrition on one side, ultra-processed overload on the other.”

“Portugal?”

“Still better than many, but moving in the wrong direction.”

“So… What Do We Actually Do?”

Philip smiles again. “We keep it simple.”

  • Stick to foods with five ingredients or fewer
  • Eat food your grandmother would recognise
  • Cook more, even if it’s basic
  • Plan ahead so convenience doesn’t beat intention
  • Remember — supplements don’t fix bad diets

They both stand there quietly for a moment.

The Portuguese finally laughs.“All these years chasing heavier weights… and the real work was in the kitchen.”

Final Thoughts

Looking back now, I wish I had fully understood then what I know today — that the kitchen is where real muscles, brains, and long-term health are built. Far more fundamental than the amount of weight one lifts at a young age.

If this small gym-side conversation reaches even a few younger men and women today, then it’s worth sharing. It’s a small contribution from my own years of muscle building, learning, and unlearning.
