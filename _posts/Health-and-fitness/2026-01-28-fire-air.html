Trás-os-Montes, January. Snow on the ground. A storm named Ingrid cutting through the mountains.

Emanuel and his wife Maria step into their small village house after a short walk home from the neighbours. It was meant to be a simple visit — coffee, conversation, and one of those traditions that never seem to age: slices of bread held on thin iron rods, toasted directly in the flames while stories flowed and hands slowly thawed.

The warmth had been comforting. Familiar. Almost grounding.

But the walk home through the snow was short and bitter, and by the time they closed the door behind them, the cold had settled deep into their bones.

As Emanuel sets two cups of hot tea on the table, Maria rubs her hands and smiles faintly.

“Sometimes I wish my parents hadn’t closed off the old fireplace here,” she says. “Like theirs. Real fire. Real heat.”

Emanuel understands exactly what she means. He sits down, wrapping his hands around his mug, and remembers a scientific article he read just a week ago — one that made him see evenings like this through a different lens.

“I get it,” he says gently. “But you also know how the smoke affects you every time we’re there.”

The conversation drifts — as good conversations do — from comfort to health, from tradition to awareness.

The Comfort of Wood Fires — and the Hidden Cost

In rural Portugal, especially regions like Trás-os-Montes, wood-burning fireplaces and stoves are still a cornerstone of winter life. They’re practical, affordable, and deeply tied to memory and identity.

But science has been quietly adding an important footnote to that picture.

When wood burns, it releases fine particulate matter known as PM2.5 — microscopic particles small enough to reach deep into the lungs and pass into the bloodstream.

Recent research highlighted by scientists at Northwestern University shows that during winter months, residential wood burning can account for more than 20% of total PM2.5 exposure in some regions.

These particles have been linked to:

  • Heart disease and strokes
  • Worsening asthma and chronic lung disease
  • Cognitive decline and increased dementia risk
  • Premature death

This isn’t about a single evening by the fire — it’s about repeated exposure over many winters.

Smoke Doesn’t Stay Where the Fire Is

One of the most surprising findings in recent research is that wood smoke doesn’t remain inside the home where it’s produced.

It drifts outside, settles into streets and valleys, and seeps back into neighbouring houses — especially on cold, still nights like the ones Ingrid has brought with her.

Even people who don’t burn wood themselves end up breathing the same air.

And those sitting closest to the fire — older adults, people with sensitive lungs, or anyone with heart conditions — often feel the effects first.

Maria always does.

What This Means for Portugal

Portugal isn’t always at the centre of air pollution debates, but winter tells a different story.

National and European data consistently show that:

  • Residential heating is one of the main sources of PM2.5 in winter
  • Wood burning often rivals or exceeds traffic pollution during cold periods
  • Small towns and rural villages are not exempt from air quality risks

With an ageing population and high rates of cardiovascular disease, the health impact of winter air pollution in Portugal deserves far more attention than it usually gets.

“So What Are We Supposed to Do?”

Emanuel finishes explaining what he read — about particles too small to see, about how smoke travels, about why Maria’s chest always feels tight after long evenings by the fire.

He’s not lecturing. Just sharing.

Maria listens quietly. Then she nods.

“I understand it,” she says after a moment. “I really do. I know times are different, and health matters.”

She looks into her tea.

“I just miss those winters. Sitting with my parents around the fire. The smell of wood, the sound of it cracking. Everyone together. It felt safe.”

Emanuel reaches for her hand.

And maybe that’s the balance we’re all trying to find — holding on to warmth and memory, while making space for what we now know.

As Emanuel pauses mid-sentence, a sharp, melodic whistle cuts through the open window.

“What is that?” he asks.

“An Amolador,” Maria says.

“A what?”

“A man on a bicycle who sharpens knives and scissors for a living. He still comes by every week.”

Emanuel shakes his head in disbelief. In this place, old trades still roam the streets, coexisting with smartphones, electric cars, and modern life. It feels magical—like living inside a conversation between the past and the present.

The Invisible Side of Tradition

But not all traditions announce themselves with a friendly whistle. Some linger invisibly in the air.

Wood-burning stoves and fireplaces, still common across Europe and Portugal in particular, are now recognized as major contributors to fine particulate pollution (PM2.5). Research shows that during colder months, residential wood smoke can account for a substantial share of local air pollution—sometimes exceeding that of traffic.

Unlike the Amolador, whose presence fades as he cycles away, wood smoke can remain suspended in the air, drifting into neighboring homes and increasing long-term health risks, including heart and lung disease.

Small Changes, Meaningful Protection

Awareness doesn’t mean giving up comfort. It means making smarter choices:

  • Using only dry, well-seasoned wood
  • Avoiding smouldering, low-airflow fires
  • Upgrading to certified low-emission stoves where possible
  • Improving ventilation and indoor air filtration
  • Being mindful of children, older adults, and sensitive lungs

Warmth should support health — not quietly work against it.

My Closing Thoughts

As a CPD-certified Fitness, Health, Wellness and Nutrition Coach, I see health as the sum of our daily environment — how we move, how we eat, how we rest, and the air we breathe every single day.

Traditions matter. Memories matter. But so does quality of life as we age.

If this winter sparks conversations like Emanuel and Maria’s, then it’s a good place to start. Awareness isn’t about fear — it’s about care.

Care for our bodies, our homes, and the people we gather around the table with.
