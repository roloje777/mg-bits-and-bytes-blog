Part 3 of the series: The Upside-Down Food Pyramid

From Policy to Place

In the previous post, we stepped back into post-war Europe and saw how nutrition policy was shaped by wartime logistics — calories, shelf life, and scale mattered more than biology.

But policy doesn’t exist in the abstract.

It lands somewhere.

In this post, we move from global thinking to a very specific place — Portugal, under Salazar — and beyond its borders, into its colonies.

Because if you want to see how food policy plays out in real life, you don’t need theory.

You need fields.

Still at the Pastelaria

We’re still sitting at the pastelaria.

The coffees are half-finished now. The lunchtime crowd is starting to drift in.

I look at my wife and say, “Do you remember those stories you used to tell me about Mozambique?”

She nods immediately.

“My father’s rice farm,” she says. “I was very young, but I remember the fields. Endless rice.”

“And do you remember,” I continue, “how your cousin told us that in Portugal, everyone planted wheat?”

She pauses.

“Yes… olive trees, almonds, cherries — those came later.”

I lean back slightly.

“That wasn’t coincidence,” I say. “That was policy.”

Salazar’s Portugal: Feeding the Mainland

Under António de Oliveira Salazar, Portugal pursued a vision of self-sufficiency.

Food security wasn’t just economic — it was political.

Wheat became the cornerstone of this strategy.

Through programs like the Campanha do Trigo, farmers across Portugal — including regions like Trás-os-Montes — were encouraged, and often pressured, to plant wheat.

Not because wheat was the most nutritious crop.

But because it was:

  • Storable
  • Transportable
  • Easy to centralise
  • Politically controllable

Diversity took a back seat.

Local crops gave way to national quotas.

Food became a matter of administration.

Trás-os-Montes: When Everyone Planted the Same Thing

“My father planted wheat,” my wife’s cousin had said. “Everyone planted wheat.”

This wasn’t nostalgia.

It was structural reality.

Traditional polyculture — olives, fruit trees, legumes — slowly disappeared in favour of monoculture.

The goal wasn’t flavour, resilience, or nutrition.

The goal was yield.

Calories first.

Everything else later.

Mozambique: Rice for the Empire

Portugal’s colonies played a different role.

While the mainland focused on wheat, Mozambique became a major producer of rice.

Rice thrived in the climate, stored well, and fit perfectly into the same carbohydrate-centric logic shaping global nutrition policy.

My wife’s memories weren’t unique.

Colonial agriculture was designed around export efficiency, not local dietary diversity.

Rice flowed outward.

Policy flowed downward.

Angola: Agriculture as Infrastructure

Angola added another layer.

Its vast land and varied climate made it suitable for multiple crops — grains, coffee, sugar.

But again, the emphasis wasn’t nutritional richness.

It was economic reliability.

Colonies functioned as agricultural infrastructure — extensions of the mainland’s food security strategy.

Protein-rich local food systems were rarely prioritised.

Calories were easier to measure.

Exporting Food Security, Not Food Diversity

This is the crucial point.

Portugal didn’t just export crops.

It exported a way of thinking about food.

A way that prioritised:

  • Grains over proteins
  • Monoculture over diversity
  • Stability over nutrition

The same logic that shaped wartime rations shaped farms, trade routes, and eventually — plates.

What was grown determined what was eaten.

From Fields to Forks

I look back at my wife.

“Wheat here. Rice there,” I say. “Different places, same idea.”

She nods slowly.

“And now,” she says, “we wonder why our diets are built around bread, pasta, and rice.”

Exactly.

These policies didn’t stay on farms.

They landed squarely on our plates.

My Perspective

As an accredited CPD Fitness, Health, Nutrition, and Wellness Coach, I see how historical food systems still shape modern health outcomes.

Understanding this history isn’t about assigning blame to farmers, families, or cultures.

It’s about recognising that many of our “normal” eating patterns were engineered for food security — not metabolic health.

Once we understand that, we can start asking better questions about how we eat today.
