November 2025.

The orange frame of my Engwe EP-2 Pro hums softly beneath me as I glide through the winding mountain paths of Trás-os-Montes, Portugal. Pine-scented air. Cold fingers. Warm legs.

My headphones are plugged into my phone. A voice is speaking calmly… scientifically… ominously.

“…the long-term effects of heavy metal pollution on humans and the environment…”

It’s the COP6 conference in Geneva, Switzerland.

My mind drifts.

The gravel path dissolves. The handlebars fade.

I’m no longer cycling.

The Stage

I’m standing under bright lights. A glass of water. A microphone.

In front of me: presidents, policymakers, scientists, environmental leaders — people who never look at their phones during talks.

Dead silence.

I clear my throat.

“Ladies and gentlemen… let’s talk about metals.”

Polite chuckle. Good start.

Why Metals Are Essential… Until They Aren’t

“First things first,” I say, pacing slowly. “Metals are not the enemy.”

I explain that iron carries oxygen, zinc activates enzymes, copper supports energy production, magnesium stabilises nerves, and selenium protects us from oxidative damage.

“Without these,” I add, “life simply doesn’t happen.”

Heads nod.

“But,” I continue, “some metals have no biological job. And worse — some pretend they do.”

Toxic Metals: The Uninvited Guests

  • Mercury
  • Lead
  • Cadmium
  • Arsenic
  • Aluminium

“These metals don’t contribute to health,” I say. “They interfere with it.”

I explain how they bind to enzymes, displace essential minerals, increase oxidative stress, disrupt hormones, impair neurological signalling, and quietly accumulate over years of low-dose exposure.

The Part Most People Miss: When Toxic Metals Substitute for Nutrients

I pause.

“This,” I say, tapping the podium, “is the part almost nobody talks about.”

I explain the concept known scientifically as ionic mimicry, or what many practitioners call the preferred mineral concept.

“Your body,” I continue, “is designed to survive — not to be perfect.”

Many enzymes are metalloenzymes, meaning they require a specific mineral to function. Under ideal conditions, the correct mineral occupies that binding site.

But when someone is deficient — due to poor diet, stress, soil depletion, illness, or chronic inflammation — the body doesn’t simply shut down.

It improvises.

Toxic metals can step in as substitutes:

  • Cadmium can replace zinc in zinc-dependent enzymes
  • Lead can replace calcium in bones
  • Aluminium can interfere where magnesium should be

The enzyme still works — but poorly.

“Imagine replacing a precision-engineered part with a bent nail,” I say. “The machine runs… but it shakes, overheats, and wears out faster.”

This explains why people with mineral deficiencies often feel functional but unwell.

It also explains something even more important.

When you aggressively remove toxic metals without replacing the missing nutrients, you’re sometimes removing the only metal keeping that enzyme operational.

The result?

People feel worse.

Why Deficiencies Lead to Accumulation

I continue.

“This is also why nutrient deficiencies lead to higher toxic metal accumulation in the first place.”

  • Low calcium → more lead stored in bone
  • Low zinc → more cadmium used in enzymes
  • Low magnesium → more aluminium interference

“The body isn’t stupid,” I say. “It’s adaptive.”

But adaptation isn’t optimisation.

Symptoms Nobody Connects

A hand goes up in the audience.

Scientist: “What kind of symptoms are we talking about?”

I smile. “The vague ones. The frustrating ones.”

  • Brain fog
  • Chronic fatigue
  • Hormonal dysfunction
  • Immune suppression
  • Cardiovascular disease
  • Developmental issues in children
  • Increased cancer risk

Where Are They Coming From?

  • Mercury: Dental amalgams, large predatory fish
  • Cadmium: Tobacco and marijuana smoke
  • Lead: Old paint, soil, and pipes
  • Arsenic: Rice and contaminated groundwater
  • Aluminium: Cookware, foil, deodorants, food cans

“Most people don’t have one big exposure,” I say. “They have thousands of tiny ones.”

Why Testing Is Tricky

Blood tests show what’s circulating—not what’s stored.

Hair tests show what’s been excreted—not what’s retained.

“Ironically,” I add, “very low readings often mean the body is a poor eliminator.”

Detox: The Three Roads

I pause and look around the room.

“This,” I say, “is where most people go wrong — not because they don’t care, but because nobody explains the options properly.”

There are three main ways people try to remove heavy metals. Each works differently. Each has a place. And each can backfire when used at the wrong time.

Road One: Chelation Therapy

“Chelation,” I explain, “comes from the Greek word for claw.”

It works by introducing a synthetic compound — such as EDTA, DMPS, or DMSA — that binds tightly to metals in the bloodstream so they can be excreted through urine.

When it makes sense:

  • Acute, high-dose exposure
  • Industrial or accidental poisoning
  • Medical emergencies under supervision

When it often causes problems:

  • Chronic, low-dose exposure
  • People with mineral deficiencies
  • Weak liver or kidney function

“Chelators don’t just grab toxic metals,” I say. “They grab everything.”

Essential minerals are lost. Enzymes lose their last functioning metal. Symptoms worsen. And if elimination pathways can’t keep up, metals get redistributed into other tissues.

“Chelation is a fire extinguisher,” I conclude. “Useful in a fire. Not for daily cooking.”

Road Two: Binders

Binders are substances that attach to metals and toxins so they can be removed, usually through bile and the digestive tract.

Examples include chlorella, activated charcoal, zeolite, humic and fulvic acids, and pectin.

“Think of binders as sponges,” I say. “They soak things up.”

When binders can be helpful:

  • Mild to moderate exposure
  • Supporting gut and bile-based elimination
  • Reducing oxidative stress during detox

When they fall short:

  • If liver and bile flow are sluggish
  • If mineral status is already poor
  • If taken too aggressively

Some binders stay in the gut. Others are small enough to enter the bloodstream.

“But here’s the catch,” I say. “If the exit doors aren’t open, all you’re doing is stirring the room.”

Quality matters too. Poorly sourced binders can arrive already contaminated.

Road Three: Mineral Balancing (My preferred method)

I slow down.

“This approach doesn’t chase metals,” I say. “It makes them unnecessary.”

Mineral balancing focuses on:

  • Restoring essential mineral levels
  • Supporting liver and kidney function
  • Improving enzyme efficiency

As preferred minerals return, toxic metals lose their role as substitutes.

The body recognises them as redundant — and lets them go.

When mineral balancing works best:

  • Chronic exposure
  • Long-standing symptoms
  • Fatigue, hormone issues, immune dysfunction

“This is slower,” I admit. “But it’s safer. And it works with physiology, not against it.”

Detox becomes a byproduct of health, not a battle.

The Supporting Nutrients

  • Selenium – supports elimination of multiple toxic metals
  • Sulfur compounds – garlic, onions, cruciferous vegetables
  • Cysteine & Glycine – glutathione synthesis
  • Zinc – blocks toxicity and increases metallothionein
  • Fiber – binds metals in bile

A Pause… and a Plate

Just as I finish the slide, the room stirs.

Plates appear. Cutlery clinks. Steam rises.

I smile.

“Perfect timing,” I say. “Because detox doesn’t happen in capsules alone. It happens on your plate.”

A Simple 3-Day Detox-Support Meal Plan

This plan feeds elimination pathways rather than forcing detox.

Breakfast

  • Eggs with spinach, onions, and garlic
  • 1–2 Brazil nuts

Lunch

  • Wild-caught salmon
  • Steamed broccoli or Brussels sprouts
  • Quinoa or brown rice

Dinner

  • Grass-fed beef, lentils, or chickpeas
  • Roasted sweet potatoes
  • Cruciferous vegetables

Snacks & Drinks

  • Berries
  • Pumpkin seeds
  • Dandelion root or milk thistle tea

Summary: What Actually Works

  • Toxic metals accumulate in tissues, not blood
  • Mineral deficiencies invite substitution by toxic metals
  • Aggressive detox without repletion can backfire
  • Liver and kidney support are foundational
  • Mineral balancing enables safe, natural elimination

Closing Thoughts

The applause fades.

I’m back on my bike.

The mountains return. The road stretches ahead.

Heavy metals are a modern environmental reality.

But the solution isn’t fear — it’s understanding how the body adapts, survives, and heals when given the right resources.

I’m a CPD-accredited fitness, health, nutrition, and wellness coach, and my work focuses on restoring foundational health so the body can do what it has always known how to do.

Pedal by pedal. Cell by cell.
