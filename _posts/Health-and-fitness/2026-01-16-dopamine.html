Aveiro, Portugal. Lunchtime. Three university students, one tray of food, and a lot of dopamine.

The Scene: University of Aveiro Cafeteria

The cafeteria buzzed with the usual lunchtime chaos — clinking cutlery, espresso machines hissing, and students arguing about exams.

At a corner table sat Carmen, Daniel, and Nicole.

  • Carmen – Psychology major. South African accent, English-speaking, curious mind, parents from the UK and Spain. Observes people the way others observe sunsets.
  • Daniel – Sports Science major. Portuguese. Dream: coaching SLB Benfica one day. Eats like he trains.
  • Nicole – Biology major. Daniel’s sister. Sharp, analytical, and the unofficial fact-checker of the group.

On the table:

  • One francesinha (because this is Portugal)
  • A plate of grilled chicken, rice, and salad
  • Sopa do dia
  • Two galões and one espresso

Their shared course? “Effects of Activities and Environment on Human Behaviour.”
Today’s topic: Dopamine.

“So… What Even Is Dopamine?”

Carmen leaned back. “Okay, psychologist version first: dopamine isn’t the ‘happy hormone’. It’s the ‘go get it’ chemical.”

Nicole nodded. “Biology agrees. It’s a neurotransmitter. Basically, a chemical messenger that helps brain cells talk to each other.”

Daniel frowned. “So… it’s not happiness?”

“Nope,” Carmen smiled. “It’s motivation. Wanting. Chasing. Anticipation.”

Daniel stabbed his chicken. “So this francesinha gave me dopamine?”

Nicole laughed. “Not exactly. Dopamine spikes more when you expect the francesinha than when you eat it.”

Daniel froze. “That explains so much about my life.”

Where Dopamine Comes From (And Why Food Matters)

Nicole slipped into lecturer mode.

“Your brain makes dopamine from an amino acid called tyrosine. You get it from protein.”

  • Eggs
  • Fish
  • Chicken
  • Dairy
  • Beans and lentils

“Then,” she continued, “tyrosine becomes L-DOPA, then dopamine. But it only works properly if you have enough iron, vitamin B6, folate, and vitamin C.”

Carmen glanced at Daniel’s plate. “Congratulations. Your lunch is neurochemically responsible.”

Daniel grinned. “Benfica coaches eat well.”

Dopamine in Real Life (AKA: Why You Check Your Phone)

“Dopamine fires when you finish a task,” Carmen said. “Or when you think you’re about to get something good.”

  • Finishing assignments
  • Exercise
  • Learning something new
  • Likes on Instagram
  • Messages

Nicole added, “The problem is overstimulation. Social media gives fast dopamine spikes. Then crashes.”

Daniel sighed. “So scrolling TikTok at 2am is… bad?”

“For your dopamine receptors?” Carmen replied sweetly. “Yes. Very.”

The Pros and Cons of Dopamine

The Good

  • Motivation
  • Focus
  • Learning
  • Feeling rewarded for effort

The Bad (When Unbalanced)

  • Addiction
  • Impulsivity
  • Low motivation when levels crash
  • Difficulty enjoying simple things

“Too little dopamine,” Nicole said, “looks like depression.”

“Too much stimulation,” Carmen added, “looks like addiction.”

Daniel blinked. “This course is personally attacking me.”

Exercise vs Meditation: The Ultimate Dopamine Debate

Daniel leaned forward. “Exercise boosts dopamine massively. Especially strength training.”

“True,” said Carmen. “But meditation stabilizes it.”

Nicole summed it up:

  • Exercise: Big dopamine release, motivation, energy
  • Meditation: Small increase, better receptor sensitivity, fewer cravings

“Meditation even reduces cortisol,” Nicole added. “And stress blocks dopamine.”

Daniel raised an eyebrow at Carmen. “You meditate?”

She smiled. “Every day. Five minutes.”

He paused. “Maybe you can teach me.”

Nicole pretended not to notice the eye contact.

Why Meditation Actually Reduces Cravings

“Dopamine is about wanting,” Carmen explained. “Meditation trains you to notice the urge without acting on it.”

Nicole nodded. “It strengthens the prefrontal cortex — self-control.”

Daniel leaned back. “So instead of fighting cravings, you just… watch them?”

“Exactly,” Carmen said. “And they lose power.”

Daniel smiled. “That’s… kind of brilliant.”

The Real Dopamine Formula

Nicole wrote it on a napkin:

Effort + Meaning + Recovery = Healthy Dopamine

  • Effort: exercise, studying, training
  • Meaning: goals, values, purpose
  • Recovery: sleep, meditation, calm

Daniel folded the napkin carefully. “I’m keeping this.”

Carmen smiled. “Good coaches understand brains.”

Dopamine, Deadlines & a Moment of Stillness

Aveiro, Portugal — late afternoon. The cafeteria is starting to empty.

“You Know What?” Carmen Said…

The plates were mostly empty now. Only coffee cups remained.

Daniel leaned back in his chair, hands behind his head. “So basically,” he said, “my brain just wants me to chase rewards like a badly trained puppy.”

Nicole rolled her eyes. “A scientifically accurate description.”

Carmen smiled, stirring her galão. “You know… we’ve talked a lot about dopamine today.”

She paused.

“But dopamine isn’t fixed by talking about it.”

Daniel raised an eyebrow. “Uh oh. This sounds like psychology.”

“It is,” she laughed. “Come meditate with me.”

Nicole blinked. “Now?”

“Now,” Carmen said. “Before your phones steal all your dopamine again.”

Daniel stood up. “If this helps me focus better at training tomorrow… I’m in.”

Leaving the Noise Behind

They walked in comfortable silence, leaving the campus buildings behind.

The path led them toward a quiet green area overlooking the hills — the kind of place students walk past without really noticing.

Carmen stopped. “Here’s good.”

They sat down.

No textbooks. No phones. No deadlines.

Just breath.

The Meditation

Carmen spoke softly.

“Dopamine spikes when we’re always chasing the next thing. Meditation teaches the brain that it’s safe to stop.”

Daniel closed his eyes. Nicole followed.

For once, no one argued.

Time slowed.

No notifications. No cravings. Just awareness.

After the Stillness

When they opened their eyes, the world felt… quieter.

Nicole stretched. “Okay. I hate that this worked.”

Daniel exhaled slowly. “I feel like my brain just stopped shouting at me.”

Carmen smiled. “That’s balanced dopamine.”

Daniel looked at her. “You should put that on a t-shirt.”

She laughed.

Walking Toward the Sunset

The sun was already dipping low, painting the sky in gold and soft orange.

They walked together, side by side.

No rush.

No need to fill the silence.

Daniel broke the quiet.

“You know,” he said, “if my players learned this… they’d train better.”

Carmen glanced at him. “Good coaches understand the brain.”

Nicole smirked. “I’m witnessing character development.”

The sun slipped below the horizon.

And for once, no one felt the urge to chase anything.

Final Takeaway

Dopamine isn’t about constant pleasure.

It’s about progress, effort, anticipation, and balance.

And sometimes… about a francesinha, good coffee, and a conversation that makes learning feel rewarding.
