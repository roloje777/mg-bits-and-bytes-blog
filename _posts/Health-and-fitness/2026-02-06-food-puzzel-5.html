Part 5 of the series: The Upside-Down Food Pyramid

From Plates to Physiology

In the previous post, we looked at how the food pyramid reshaped our plates — placing carbohydrates at the centre and pushing protein to the margins.

But diagrams don’t have blood sugar.

People do.

In this post, we move from policy and habit into biology — into muscle, aging, and the quiet assumptions we’ve carried forward for decades.

After Lunch, Over a Bica

We’ve just finished lunch.

The plates are cleared. The kitchen is calm again.

We’re standing by the counter, each holding a bica.

“How I miss having the children here,” my wife says softly.

She’s thinking about when my son and daughter-in-law came all the way from South Africa for the holidays.

“Yes,” I reply, “it was great. I hope they repeat it soon. I miss them terribly.”

There’s a pause.

Then she smiles.

“Talking about the food pyramid,” she says, “do you remember that heated debate you had with Carmen?”

I laugh.

How could I forget?

The Question That Always Comes Up

“Carbs versus protein,” my wife continues. “Carmen asked you — health-wise — if you had to pick just one.”

I nod.

“She said it had to be carbohydrates,” I reply. “And I said no — I’d pick protein. And probably a little fat above carbs.”

At the time, it sounded provocative.

But it wasn’t ideology.

It was biology.

Protein Is Not Just Calories

One of the biggest mistakes in nutrition is treating protein as interchangeable with carbohydrates.

They’re not.

Carbohydrates are primarily energy.

Protein is structure.

Every cell, enzyme, hormone, and muscle fibre depends on amino acids — the building blocks of protein.

And unlike carbohydrates, the body does not store protein efficiently.

You either eat enough.

Or you break down your own tissue.

Not All Proteins Are Equal

This is where the story gets uncomfortable.

Protein quality matters.

Bioavailability matters.

Amino acid composition matters.

Animal-based proteins generally provide:

  1. Complete amino acid profiles
  2. Higher leucine content
  3. Greater digestibility

Plant proteins can be part of a healthy diet — but often require careful combination and larger quantities to achieve the same biological effect.

The food pyramid never made this distinction.

It counted grams.

Not outcomes.

Bioavailability in protein digestion is the proportion of amino acids from ingested protein that are actually digested, absorbed, and utilized by the body for metabolic functions, rather than being wasted.

It determines how effectively a protein source is used for tissue repair and muscle building, with animal proteins (e.g., whey, eggs) typically having higher bioavailability than plant-based sources.

Muscle: The Metabolic Organ We Ignored

For decades, muscle was seen as cosmetic.

Something athletes cared about.

But muscle is one of the most important metabolic organs in the body.

It:

  1. Improves insulin sensitivity
  2. Acts as a glucose sink
  3. Supports mobility and independence
  4. Protects against frailty

And muscle is built — and maintained — with protein.

Especially as we age.

Aging Changes the Rules

Older adults require more protein, not less.

Yet most guidelines still reflect minimums established for young, active men during wartime.

This mismatch has consequences:

  1. Sarcopenia
  2. Loss of strength
  3. Increased fall risk
  4. Metabolic decline

Eating “enough” protein on paper doesn't mean eating enough for your biology.

The Quiet Cost of Getting It Wrong

When protein is sidelined, the body adapts — but at a cost.

Muscle slowly erodes.

Blood sugar control worsens.

Recovery slows.

And we call it aging.

Often, it's undernutrition.
