ATP

Adenosine triphosphate (ATP) is a molecule that stores and releases energy in cells.
ATP is made up of adenine, ribose, and three phosphate groups.
The bonds between the phosphate groups store energy that is released when the bonds are broken.
This energy powers cellular processes like muscle contraction, nerve impulses, and chemical synthesis.
It's often called the "energy currency" of the cell.

Oxygen is used in Cellular Respiration

You breathe in oxygen through your nose and mouth.
Oxygen is transported to cells by hemoglobin in red blood cells.
Oxygen is released into cells, where it's used in cellular respiration.
Cells combine glucose and oxygen to produce ATP and carbon dioxide.

How oxygen is used in muscles

Muscles require more oxygen when they work harder. The more energy muscles need, the more oxygen they require.

What is VO2Max

It s the maximum amount of oxygen your body can use during exercise.
It's a key indicator of your aerobic fitness and cardiovascular endurance.
The more oxygen your body can consume, the more energy you can generate.
The more efficiently your body uses oxygen, the better you can handle activities that require a lot of oxygen, like running and swimming.
    This is how one can use the resting heart rate:
  • To gauge your current and future health
  • To track your aerobic fitness level
  • To see if you're fit for training, overtrained, or stressed
  • To identify potential health problems
  • To track your progress over time
  • To help you hit your target heart rate during exercise

Cardio

The traditional view is that if you want to lose fat, you should do more cardio, and that if you want to build muscle, you should hit the gym and lift heavy weights.

Resistance and Weight Training

A normal weight lifting routine will only burn around two to 300 calories per session, maybe a bit more .

The Afterburn Effect

The afterburn effect, also known as excess post-exercise oxygen consumption (EPOC), is the increased rate of oxygen and calorie consumption after a workout.
It's the energy your body uses to return to a resting state.
In other words, Its the energy your body uses to recover after a session of cardio or weightlifting.
It's a known fact that resistance training afterburn effect is higher than that of cardio.
Even though this is true, the overall energy consumed is still higher in cardio than a session of weightlifting.
So the bottom line in terms of calories burned is that cardio is better than wait lifting.

Designing your HIIT Workout

    So when designing your HIIT workout, you need to do two things.

HIIT - High Intensity Interval Training

Now that we know how to implement this Fat Burning Exercise Routine let's look at why it works.
Let's see how high-intensity interval training (HIIT) can help your body use fat for energy instead of carbohydrates, and it can increase the rate at which you burn fat.

    How HITT Uses Fat:
  • HIIT uses the body's energy reserves, which include fat.
  • HIIT elevates your metabolism, which can burn calories for hours after you finish exercising.
  • During the recovery period, your body taps into fat stores to restore itself to its normal resting state.
  • HIIT can help you build muscle, so its great companion to muscle training
  • HIIT can lower your blood pressure and heart rate.
  • HIIT can improve your stroke volume and oxidative capacity of muscle.
  • HIIT improves your aerobic performance.
    HIIT and weight loss:
  • Studies have shown that HIIT can reduce body fat percentage and waist and hip circumference.
    HIIT and metabolism:
  • HIIT can shift your body's metabolism toward using fat for energy instead of carbohydrates.
  • It can also make your metabolism more flexible, so it can switch between carbs and fats as a fuel source.
  • High-intensity interval training (HIIT) depletes muscle glycogen stores, which are the body's emergency fuel reserve.
  • This depletion can help with weight loss and improve fitness and metabolic function.
    How HIIT depletes glycogen:
  • HIIT can deplete glycogen stores in less than 10 minutes.
  • HIIT can completely deplete glycogen in just two maximal effort sprints.
  • HIIT uses muscle glycogen at such a rate that the body increases the number and activity of mitochondria in muscles.
    How HIIT improves fitness:
  • HIIT can improve cardiovascular fitness, VO2max, and endurance capacity.
  • HIIT can also trigger a release of human growth hormone and testosterone, both of which play a key role in metabolizing fat.
