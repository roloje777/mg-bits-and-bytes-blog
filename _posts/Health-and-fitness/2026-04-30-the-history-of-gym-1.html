At Tait's Gym

The Day Joseph Schooled Us

Welkom, 1982. We're sitting in the gym's locker room, just above the Horseshoe Diner. My brother Jairo, our pal Rui, and I (the "old man" at 17) are shaking in our sneakers at Tait's Gym. We're here to get huge. Then comes Joseph—a mountain of a man with a smile that puts us right at ease.

Joseph: "Welcome, young men. Taking responsibility for your physics? Good. But tell me... are you as dedicated to your books as you are to these weights?"

Me: "Uh, we're so-so, Joseph. We just want the muscles."

Joseph: "No good! Gym people need strong brains, not just strong muscles."

Rui: "But Joseph, in the movies, the muscle guys are 'Jocks.' They’re always the dumb ones! I don't want to be a muscle-bound jock."

Thinking back today, I believe our perceptions as young boys could have been influenced by all the Archie comics we used to consume. I'm sure Big Moose had a role to play in Rui's thinking here.😂

Big Moose from the Archies

1. The Greek "Naked" Truth

Joseph laughed, a deep sound that echoed off the iron plates. "Rui, have you heard of Plato or Aristotle?" We nodded. Of course.

Joseph: "They taught in the Gymnasion. In Ancient Greece, it wasn’t just about lifting stones. It was a place where you trained the body and the mind together. And the word? It comes from gymnos.
It means 'naked.' They trained in the nude to celebrate the human form as a gift of balance."

Gymnos - training in the nude

2. Romans and the Religious "Heavy Lift"

Juvenal - MENS sana in CORPORE sano

He saw our jaws drop. "The Romans took it further," he continued. "Mens sana in corpore sano—a healthy mind in a healthy body. But then, history got a bit... heavy."

He explained how, for centuries, certain religious views started seeing the body as a distraction. "In the Middle Ages, people thought if you focused too much on your muscles, you were neglecting your soul. The body was a 'cage' for the spirit. It took a long time for people to realize that the body is actually a temple that needs maintenance!"

  • Platonic Dualism:

    Even before Christianity, Plato described the body as the 'prison of the soul.' He argued that the senses were deceptive and that true reality could only be reached through the intellect and spirit.

  • Gnosticism:

    These early movements took the idea to the extreme, teaching that the material world (including the body) was inherently flawed or evil, while the spirit was divine and good.

  • Christian Asceticism:

    Especially during the Middle Ages, the body was often viewed as the source of temptation and 'original sin.' Practices like extreme fasting or celibacy were ways to 'punish' the flesh to elevate the soul.

  • The Cartesian Split:

    In the 17th century, René Descartes solidified this separation by defining the body as a biological 'machine' distinct from the res cogitans (the thinking mind). This framed humans as minds that simply 'inhabit' bodies.

3. The Language Divide (English vs. Afrikaans)

Joseph: "Are you in an English or Afrikaans school?"

Jairo: "English, Joseph."

Joseph: "Ah, that explains why you think 'Gym' only means weights."

Joseph broke it down: In the German and Dutch traditions (which influenced the Afrikaans Gimnasium), a Gymnasium is an elite academic school. In English, we shortened it to 'Gym' and forgot the books. "The Afrikaans schools kept the academic prestige in the name; the English world kept the sweat."

The meaning of the word Gym

4. The Revival: 19th Century to Now

Jan Friedrich - the father of Gymnastics

Joseph told us about the 1800s, when people like Friedrich Jahn (The father of gymnastics) in Germany brought back "Physical Culture" to make nations strong again. Then came the 20th century, and gyms became businesses. "Now," he said, "science is finally catching up to the Greeks."

He explained that today—well, our tomorrow—science shows that when you train, your brain releases 'fertilizer' (BDNF - Brain-Derived Neurotrophic Factor) that actually makes you smarter. "You aren't a 'dumb jock' if you're doing it right; you're a high-performance machine."

    Here is how that 'fertilizer' actually works:
  • Neurogenesis:

    It triggers the birth of new neurons, particularly in the hippocampus—the part of your brain responsible for memory and learning.

  • Neural Plasticity:

    It strengthens the connections (synapses) between neurons, making it easier for them to communicate. This is why you often feel sharper after a workout.

  • Neuroprotection:

    It acts like a shield, protecting existing brain cells from damage and age-related decline.

  • The Exercise Link:

    High-intensity interval training (HIIT) and consistent aerobic exercise (like running or swimming) are the most effective ways to spike your BDNF levels.

    • In short, physical activity literally prepares your brain to learn new things and handle stress better.

Me: "What about guys like Charles Atlas or Arnold Schwarzenegger? What’s their role in all this?"

Joseph: (Smiling) "Ah, the icons. That, my friends, is a conversation for another day. Today, you learn theory. Because a muscle starts with a strong mind. Now, let's look at those books before we touch the iron."

Conclusion

Joseph taught us that day that Tait’s Gym wasn't just a place to get 'buff.' It was a bridge to a legacy of human excellence that started thousands of years ago. Whether you call it a Gimnasium or a Gym, the goal is the same: Don't let your brain atrophy while your biceps grow.

From the early gymnasiums of Ancient Greece to modern scientific terminology, 'gymn' reflects the raw essentials of progress and development.

From my side: Looking back from 2026, Joseph was a man ahead of his time. I share this story now not just as a memory, but as a CPD Fitness, Health, Wellness, and Nutrition Coach. We must return to that Greek ideal: training the body to sharpen the mind.
