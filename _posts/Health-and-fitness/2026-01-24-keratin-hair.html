Welkom, South Africa. A warm afternoon.
The hum of hair dryers.
The smell of shampoo. And one very honest hair conversation.

Setting the Scene

Roxanne sat in the salon chair, staring at her reflection. Her black hair framed her face, her big brown eyes looked fabulous as always… but her hair?

She frowned.

“James,” she said, watching him section her hair with the seriousness of a man defusing a bomb, “be honest with me. Has my hair always been this… crunchy?”

James laughed. He had been Roxanne’s hairdresser for years and knew when it was time for honesty.

“Crunchy is a strong word,” he said. “I’d say… brittle.
Your hair is trying its best, but it’s lacking something.”

The Hairdresser Drops Some Science

Roxanne raised an eyebrow. “Lacking something? Like conditioner? Or a miracle?”

James shook his head. “Neither. What you’re dealing with is a keratin issue.”

Seeing her blank stare, he continued.

“Keratin is the protein that makes your hair strong. If your body isn’t making enough of it—or isn’t making good-quality keratin—your hair becomes dry, weak, and breaks easily. No shampoo can fix that.”

“So you’re saying this is an inside job?” Roxanne asked.

“Exactly,” James said. “Your hair is basically snitching on your nutrition.”

How Hair Is Actually Made

James explained while trimming:

  • You don’t eat keratin directly.
  • Your body makes keratin inside hair follicles.
  • To do that, it needs protein, sulfur, and a few key nutrients.
  • If any step is missing, hair suffers first.

“Hair is dramatic like that,” James added. “It’s always the first thing to complain.”

The Real Reasons Hair Becomes Weak

According to James (and science):

  • Not enough high-quality protein
  • Low sulfur intake (essential for strong hair bonds)
  • Biotin deficiency
  • Low zinc or vitamin D
  • Too little—or too much—vitamin A
  • Stress, poor sleep, or hormonal imbalance

Roxanne sighed. “So basically… adulthood.”

James nodded. “Exactly.”

The 5 Nutrients Your Hair Is Begging For

1. Biotin (Hair’s Best Friend)

Helps hair-producing cells work efficiently. Even mild deficiency can cause brittle hair and shedding.

2. Cysteine (The Sulfur Hero)

This amino acid provides sulfur, which forms strong bonds that make hair tough and resilient.

3. Zinc (The Builder)

Activates enzymes that build keratin and supports hair growth signals.

4. Vitamin D (The Growth Manager)

Supports the hair growth cycle and healthy follicles.

5. Vitamin A (The Regulator)

Helps control keratin production—but balance is critical. Too much can cause hair loss.

Best Foods to Strengthen Hair Naturally

James leaned in. “If your plate looks like this, your hair will behave.”

  • Eggs (especially yolks) – protein, biotin, vitamin A
  • Meat & poultry – cysteine, zinc, complete protein
  • Fish – protein and vitamin D
  • Dairy – protein and zinc
  • Liver (small amounts) – vitamin A and biotin
  • Cod liver oil – vitamin A and D

A Simple Hair-Strength Meal Plan

  • Breakfast: Eggs with dairy or yogurt
  • Lunch: Chicken or beef with vegetables
  • Dinner: Fish with a balanced side
  • Weekly: Small serving of liver or cod liver oil

The Keratin Support Checklist

  • ✔ Eat enough protein daily
  • ✔ Include sulfur-rich foods
  • ✔ Ensure biotin and zinc intake
  • ✔ Maintain healthy vitamin D levels
  • ✔ Don’t overdo vitamin A
  • ✔ Sleep well and manage stress

The Big Reveal

James spun the chair around. Roxanne smiled.

“So,” she said, touching her hair, “you’re telling me my glow-up starts in the kitchen?”

James grinned. “Exactly. Hair is built, not bought.”

And with that, Roxanne left the salon lighter, wiser, and with a grocery list that might just save her hair.
