Do you know what happened 440 years ago, on October 12, 1582?
 Nothing happened, nothing at all. Simply because that day never existed. 
In fact, 10 full days were deleted from the month of October of that year. 
A month with only 21 days and a lot of confusion . And all this trouble was generated by the stars.


When we say that astronomy is the first of science, calendars are proof of that. In several different places on the globe, calendars appeared thousands of years ago, all of them based on astronomical cycles of the Sun, Moon or both at the same time. 
Thus, the observation of the stars was of fundamental importance for the definition of calendars.
In Rome, considered the cradle of Western civilization, a lunisolar calendar was adopted, that is, based on the solar and lunar cycles. The months began at the New Moon and the years at the spring equinox. Each week had 7 days and was dominated by a lunar phase.


 But this calendar was very problematic mainly because the lunar and solar cycles didn't fit together. This required adding extra months, with varying amounts of days, to complete the year. A confusion that was only resolved in the year 46 BC, with the adoption of the Julian Calendar.
 
 The Julian calendar adopted a year of 365 days and 6 hours, divided into 12 months of 30 or 31 days, in addition to February, with 28, but which gained an extra day every 4 years. This solved the calendar issue, but there was still one problem: the year doesn't have exactly 365 days and 6 hours.

This difference accumulated over the centuries caused the Julian calendar to reach the year 1582 with an error of more than 10 days.

Gregory assembled a group of astronomers and scientists to correct the Julian calendar.
 The goal was to make the spring equinox occur again on March 21st and correct the 10-day error that existed at the time. The commission prepared a proposal that was evaluated by princes and mathematicians and after 5 years of studies, the papal bull Inter Gravissimas was promulgated in 1582, instituting the Gregorian calendar.
