Setting the Stage

Late afternoon light spills through the window of a quiet sitting room.

A five-star general sits motionless in an armchair. His uniform jacket hangs untouched over the back. His face looks older than it did yesterday — as if ten years have passed overnight.

On the small table in front of him lies a folded newspaper. The headline is impossible to ignore:

“U.S. Forces Withdraw After Heavy Fighting in Tunisia”

In the kitchen, his wife is making dinner. She pauses, looks through the open doorway, and immediately sees the weight on his face.

The phone rings.

She answers.

“Love… it’s for you. It’s the President.”

Hours later, well past midnight, the general finally climbs into bed.

“Are you alright?” she asks.

He stares at the ceiling. “I shouldn’t be here. I should be at the office. We’re in trouble.”

She turns toward him. Calm. Certain.

“No. You should rest. You must put urgent things aside and put important things first.”

He frowns. “What do you mean?”

She smiles. “For one, before you leave in the morning, take out the garbage. If you don’t, it’ll fill the kitchen with flies.”

He is quiet for a long moment.

Then he smiles.

“Thank you, my love.”

“For what?”

“For saving the world.”

She laughs. “I did what?”

He turns off the lamp.

“What is important is seldom urgent, and what is urgent is seldom important.”

And with that, Dwight D. Eisenhower goes to sleep.

The Next Morning

Early morning. The Pentagon.

The general walks into his office. His secretary is already there.

“Good morning, General. Coffee?”

“Yes, please. And gather all department heads in the planning room. I’ll need a flip chart… and four pens. Different colours.”

Moments later, silence fills the room as he draws a simple 2×2 matrix.

He labels each box.

He turns to the room.

“Gentlemen…”

The plan begins.

From Eisenhower to Covey

The idea behind this matrix is often called the Eisenhower Matrix, though Eisenhower himself never drew it quite this way.

His insight was simple and brutal:

  • Urgent things demand attention.
  • Important things demand judgment.
  • The two are rarely the same.

Decades later, Stephen R. Covey refined this thinking into what we now know as the FranklinCovey Time Matrix, a practical tool used in leadership training, business strategy, and personal productivity.

The four quadrants force one uncomfortable question:

“Where is my time actually going?”

Not Just for Generals

You don’t need to command armies for this to matter.

The same logic applies to:

  • your inbox
  • your calendar
  • your phone notifications
  • your evenings
  • your weekends

Most stress comes from living in Quadrant I. Important and Urgent

Most frustration comes from Quadrant III. Not Important and Urgent

Most regret lives quietly in Quadrant IV.Not Important and Not Urgent

And most growth — real, lasting growth — happens in Quadrant II - Important and Not Urgent.

Why This Still Matters Today

The world is louder than it was in Eisenhower’s time.

Urgency now fits in your pocket, buzzes on your wrist, and follows you into bed.

The matrix is not about doing less. It’s about doing what matters before it becomes a crisis.

Or, put differently:

Take out the garbage before it fills the kitchen with flies.
